# Meeting the Review Family: Exploring Review Types and Associated Information Retrieval Requirements

## Publication Information
- **Title**: Meeting the review family: exploring review types and associated information retrieval requirements
- **Authors**: Anthea Sutton, Mark Clowes, Louise Preston, Andrew Booth
- **Published**: 2026-05-04
- **Journal**: Health Information Research
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.1111/hir.12276

## Abstract Summary
This study characterizes health-related literature reviews by type and provides recommendations on appropriate information retrieval methods based on available guidance. Forty-eight review types were identified and categorized into seven families, revealing that while published guidance shows increasing specification of search methods, much remains generic with many review types lacking explicit requirements for evidence identification.

## Seven Review Families Identified

### 1. Traditional Reviews
- **Critical review**: Demonstrates extensive research and critical evaluation, typically results in hypothesis or model
- **Integrative Review (Integrative Synthesis)**: Umbrella term for synthesis methods integrating qualitative and quantitative data
- **Narrative review**: Conventional literature review, particularly when contrasted with systematic review
- **Narrative summary**: Overview of available evidence addressing research questions, often produced within short timeframe
- **State of the art review**: Addresses current matters, may offer new perspectives or point out areas for further research

### 2. Systematic Reviews
- **Cochrane review of effects**: Systematic summaries of healthcare intervention effects
- **Comparative effectiveness review**: Depicts relative benefits and harms of range of options
- **Diagnostic Systematic Review (Diagnostic Test Accuracy Review)**: Summarizes evidence about test accuracy
- **Meta-analysis**: Statistically combines results of quantitative studies for more precise effect
- **Network meta-analysis**: Network structure connecting treatments compared in RCTs
- **Prognostic review**: Determines overall prognosis and link between prognostic factors and outcomes
- **Psychometric review**: Evaluates psychometric properties of tests (reliability and validity)
- **Review of economic evaluations**: Identifies, measures, values and compares costs and outcomes
- **Systematic review**: Systematically searches for, appraises and synthesizes research evidence
- **Systematic review of Epidemiology Studies**: Determines prevalence/incidence or etiology/risk

### 3. Review of Reviews
- **Review of Reviews (Overview)**: Generic summary surveying literature characteristics; Cochrane Overviews summarize multiple intervention reviews
- **Umbrella review**: Compiles evidence from multiple reviews into one accessible document

### 4. Rapid Reviews
- **Rapid Review (Rapid Evidence Synthesis)**: Simplifies or omits systematic review components for short timeframe
- **Rapid evidence assessment**: Faster and less rigorous than full systematic review but more rigorous than ad hoc searching
- **Rapid Realist Synthesis (Rapid Realist Review)**: Applies realist approach to produce policy-relevant product within limited time/resources

### 5. Qualitative Reviews (Experiential Reviews)
- **Qualitative Evidence Synthesis (QES)**: Broad term for systematic reviews of qualitative research evidence; looks for themes or constructs across studies
- **Qualitative Interpretive Meta-synthesis**: Generates in-depth understanding to develop theory and inform practice/policy
- **Qualitative meta-synthesis**: Intentional approach to analyzing data across qualitative studies
- **Qualitative research synthesis**: Relies on sophisticated interpretivist methods, developed from efforts to offer synthesis methods with increasing specificity

## Key Findings and Recommendations

### Key Messages
1. Use consistent terminology when referring to review types and review families, making clear distinction between review types within the same family
2. Information specialists should be familiar with review families & types and associated retrieval methods to enhance their role with the review team
3. Where generic methods are not appropriate, information specialists should develop (and validate) specific methods for evidence identification suitable for each review type
4. Definitions of (and practical guidance on) iterative searching are required
5. Further validation of automated methods to support evidence identification is required

### Methodological Guidance Sources Searched
The study searched websites of 18 major review-producing organizations including:
- Cochrane, Campbell Collaboration, Centre for Reviews and Dissemination (University of York)
- Joanna Briggs Institute, AHRQ, NICE, NIHR
- CADTH, ISPOR, HTAi, and others

### Guidance Classification
Types of guidance were classified as:
1. **Official guidance** – Produced by recognized organizations that generate or commission reviews
2. **Methodological advice** – Peer-reviewed publications by authors with review experience
3. **Current practice** – Case studies, conference presentations, or online resources
