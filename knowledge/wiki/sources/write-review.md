# How to Write a Literature Review Paper?

## Publication Information
- **Title**: How to Write a Literature Review Paper?
- **Authors**: David Banister, Bert Van Wee
- **Published**: 2016-03-03
- **Journal**: Transport Reviews
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.1080/01441647.2015.1065456

## Abstract Summary
This paper discusses how to write a literature review paper (LRP), stressing the primary importance of adding value rather than only providing an overview. It presents different types of LRPs, advises on reporting methodology for paper selection, and outlines the structure of an LRP. The authors conclude that heterogeneity in LRPs is very large and there is no standard template.

## Key Guidance Points
### Rationale and Added Value
- **Added Value**: A review must add value (e.g., explicit research gaps, methodological advantages/disadvantages, implications). An overview paper does not need to add value, but a review paper does.
- **Benefits**: High-quality reviews can be heavily cited, build reputation, and help position research in the academic literature. They are useful for teaching and refreshing knowledge bases.
- **Risks**: Reviews may be perceived as less valuable than empirical papers due to lack of "original research" or confusion between overview and critical review types.

### Types of LRPs
- **Overview Paper**: General summary, does not necessarily add value.
- **Critical Review**: Adds value through analysis, conceptual innovation, or new frameworks.
- **Theme Papers**: Focus on a specific theme rather than all literature in an area (e.g., low-carbon transport systems).
- **Conceptual Model Papers**: Present a model first, then explore supporting literature.

### Methodology: Selection of Papers
- **Explicitness**: Authors should be explicit about methodologies used for selection. Many LRPs lack this.
- **Databases**: Common sources include Web of Science, SCOPUS, Scholar Google, TRID. Search strings and Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT) must be reported.
- **Snowballing**: Forward (citations to a paper) and backward (citations in a paper) snowballing should be made explicit if used.
- **Thresholds**: Minimum threshold often around 30 papers; unlikely to exceed 100 in transport fields. Stratified selection is acceptable if scope is too large.
- **Context**: Limitations regarding language, time frame, or geographical context should be justified (e.g., land use impacts differ between USA and EU).

### Structure and Writing
- **Introduction**: Discuss background, known lines of enquiry, gaps, motivation, research questions, and target audience.
- **Methodology Section**: Should detail search strategy, databases, keywords, time frames, and selection criteria.
- **Avoiding Pitfalls**: Do not criticize original authors for methods that were state-of-the-art at the time. Avoid simple averaging of quantitative results without weighting or context.
- **Conclusions**: Must provide clear interpretation and discussion on the "why" behind results, even if speculative (made explicit).
