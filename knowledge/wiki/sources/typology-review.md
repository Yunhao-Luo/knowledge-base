# A Typology of Reviews: An Analysis of 14 Review Types and Associated Methodologies

## Publication Information
- **Title**: A typology of reviews: an analysis of 14 review types and associated methodologies
- **Authors**: Maria J. Grant, Andrew Booth
- **Published**: 2009
- **Journal**: Health Information and Libraries Journal
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1471-1842.2009.00848.x

## Abstract Summary
This study provides descriptive insight into the most common types of reviews, with illustrative examples from health and health information domains. The authors examine vocabulary associated with review literature and map 14 review types against a Search, Appraisal, Synthesis and Analysis (SALSA) framework. Few review types possess prescribed and explicit methodologies, and many fall short of being mutually exclusive.

## Background Context
- Evidence-based practice (EBP) emerged in early 1990s
- Archie Cochrane advocated for critical summaries of randomized controlled trials
- Cochrane Collaboration formed in 1992 to provide systematic reviews
- Library and information sector adopted systematic review methods after EBLIP conference (2001)
- Terminology confusion exists: "review of the evidence," "comprehensive review," "literature review," "overview," "systematic review"

## The SALSA Framework
Four methodological components used to analyze review types:
1. **Search** - Completeness and comprehensiveness of literature search
2. **Appraisal** - Quality assessment of included studies
3. **Synthesis** - How findings are combined (narrative, tabular, graphical)
4. **Analysis** - Type of analysis performed (thematic, meta-analysis, descriptive, etc.)

## Fourteen Review Types Identified

### 1. Critical Review
- Aims to demonstrate extensive research and critical evaluation
- Goes beyond description to include analysis and conceptual innovation
- Typically results in hypothesis or model
- **Search**: Identifies most significant items in the field
- **Appraisal**: No formal quality assessment; evaluates by contribution
- **Synthesis**: Narrative, conceptual, or chronological
- **Analysis**: Identifies conceptual contribution to embody existing or derive new theory

### 2. Literature Review
- Generic term for published materials examining recent/current literature
- May or may not include comprehensive searching or quality assessment
- Synthesis typically narrative; analysis may be chronological, conceptual, thematic

### 3. Mapping Review / Systematic Map
- Maps and categorizes existing literature to identify gaps
- **Search**: Completeness determined by time/scope constraints
- **Appraisal**: No formal quality assessment
- **Synthesis**: May be graphical and tabular
- **Analysis**: Characterizes quantity and quality of literature, identifies need for primary/secondary research

### 4. Meta-analysis
- Statistically combines results of quantitative studies for more precise effect
- **Search**: Exhaustive, comprehensive searching; may use funnel plot
- **Appraisal**: Quality assessment determines inclusion/exclusion and/or sensitivity analyses
- **Synthesis**: Graphical and tabular with narrative commentary
- **Analysis**: Numerical analysis of measures of effect assuming absence of heterogeneity

### 5. Mixed Studies Review / Mixed Methods Review
- Combines quantitative and qualitative research or outcome with process studies
- **Search**: Requires sensitive search to retrieve all studies or separate strategies
- **Appraisal**: Generic appraisal instrument or separate processes with checklists
- **Synthesis**: Both components presented as narrative and tables; may use graphical integration
- **Analysis**: Characterizes both literatures, looks for correlations or gap analysis

### 6. Overview
- Generic term: summary of literature attempting to survey characteristics
- May or may not include comprehensive searching or quality assessment
- Synthesis depends on whether systematic or not; typically narrative with tabular features
- Analysis may be chronological, conceptual, thematic

### 7. Qualitative Systematic Review / Qualitative Evidence Synthesis
- Integrates or compares findings from qualitative studies
- Looks for 'themes' or 'constructs' across individual qualitative studies
- **Search**: May employ selective or purposive sampling
- **Appraisal**: Quality assessment used to mediate messages, not for inclusion/exclusion
- **Synthesis**: Qualitative, narrative synthesis
- **Analysis**: Thematic analysis, may include conceptual models

### 8. Rapid Review
- Assessment of what is known about policy/practice issue using systematic review methods
- **Search**: Completeness determined by time constraints
- **Appraisal**: Time-limited formal quality assessment
- **Synthesis**: Typically narrative and tabular
- **Analysis**: Quantities of literature and overall quality/direction of effect

### 9. Scoping Review
- Preliminary assessment of potential size and scope of available research literature
- Aims to identify nature and extent of research evidence (usually including ongoing research)
- **Search**: Completeness determined by time/scope constraints; may include research in progress
- **Appraisal**: No formal quality assessment
- **Synthesis**: Typically tabular with some narrative commentary
- **Analysis**: Characterizes quantity and quality of literature, specifies viable review

### 10. State-of-the-art Review
- Addresses current matters in contrast to combined retrospective and current approaches
- May offer new perspectives or point out areas for further research
- **Search**: Aims for comprehensive searching of current literature
- **Appraisal**: No formal quality assessment
- **Synthesis**: Typically narrative, may have tabular accompaniment
- **Analysis**: Current state of knowledge and priorities for future investigation

### 11. Systematic Review
- Seeks to systematically search for, appraise and synthesize research evidence
- Often adheres to guidelines on conduct of review
- **Search**: Exhaustive, comprehensive searching
- **Appraisal**: Quality assessment may determine inclusion/exclusion
- **Synthesis**: Typically narrative with tabular accompaniment
- **Analysis**: What is known; recommendations for practice. What remains unknown; uncertainty around findings

### 12. Systematic Search and Review
- Combines strengths of critical review with comprehensive search process
- Addresses broad questions to produce 'best evidence synthesis'
- **Search**: Exhaustive, comprehensive searching
- **Appraisal**: May or may not include quality assessment
- **Synthesis**: Minimal narrative, tabular summary of studies
- **Analysis**: What is known; recommendations for practice. Limitations

### 13. Systematized Review
- Attempts to include elements of systematic review process while stopping short
- Typically conducted as postgraduate student assignment
- **Search**: May or may not include comprehensive searching
- **Appraisal**: May or may not include quality assessment
- **Synthesis**: Typically narrative with tabular accompaniment
- **Analysis**: What is known; uncertainty around findings; limitations of methodology

### 14. Umbrella Review
- Compiles evidence from multiple reviews into one accessible document
- Focuses on broad condition/problem with competing interventions
- **Search**: Identification of component reviews, no search for primary studies
- **Appraisal**: Quality assessment of studies within component reviews and/or of reviews themselves
- **Synthesis**: Graphical and tabular with narrative commentary
- **Analysis**: What is known; recommendations for practice. What remains unknown

## Key Findings
1. Few review types possess prescribed and explicit methodologies
2. Many review types fall short of being mutually exclusive
3. Terminology confusion exists across health information domain
4. Limited number of review types currently utilized within health information domain
5. Typology provides valuable reference point for commissioning, conducting, supporting or interpreting reviews
